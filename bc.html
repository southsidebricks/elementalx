Emails

How to write professional correspondence

In the professional world, the majority of communication is done through email. Therefore, it is very important to know how to structure, write, and sound in an email to a manager or coworker.

There are 5 parts of an email that you'll need to remember:

  1. Subject line (with initial caps)
  2. Greeting (with correct punctuation)
  3. Body (at least 5 sentences)
  4. Closing (with correct punctuation)
  5. Signature (correctly formatted in rich text)

The following is an example of a professional email and and an UNprofessional email. Pay close attention to details.


To: gchertavian@yearup.org

CC:

Subject: Friday's Visit

Hello Gerald,

I truly appreciated you visiting our site this past Friday. You shared many thoughts and experiences that were inspiring, like when you mentioned how difficult it was to get your first investor.

Your devotion to closing the opportunity divide is remarkable and I hope to make an impact in the same way that you have impacted so many students.

Thank you,

O'Shea Jackson
Year Up Student
Year Up - Chicago
223 W Jackson, Suite 400
Chicago, IL 60606

Which of the 5 parts of an email are missing, and why?

  1. Subject line (with initial caps)
  2. Greeting (with correct punctuation)
  3. Body (at least 5 sentences)
  4. Closing (with correct punctuation)
  5. Signature (correctly formatted in rich text)

It is important to remember that people can judge you based on a single email. Always make sure you are presenting your best professional self!


Apart from good grammar and the 5 parts of the email, something very important to monitor is your tone. As excited as you may be, stay away from exclamation points, CAPS LOCK, and jokes. Be clear, concise, and direct.


Why?


Tone is difficult to interpet. People can think you mean something else, there can be misinterpretations, and who knows if someone is having a bad day? You don't want to be caught in a wrong-place-wrong-time situation with a busy supervisor.

Grammar

Parts of Speech

In this section, we'll touch upon grammar rules that you may have heard before. Make sure you really understand each one, because you'll be quizzed!

Nouns:
Person, place, and thing. However, there are common nouns, proper nouns and pronouns!

Common Noun: a general person, place, thing, or idea.
Proper Noun: a specific person, place, thing, or idea.
Pronoun: a replacement for proper and common nouns, like himself, herself, his, her, they, and it.

Example Sentence: Identify the common, proper, and pronoun.
"Max, the meerkat, had a great time while dancing by himself at the club."

The answer is:

Max is the Proper Noun,
meerkat is the Common Noun, and
himself is the Pronoun.

Verbs:
Actions! Make sure you understand adverbs, verb phrases, helping verbs, linking verbs, and action verbs.

Adverb: A word that describes a verb, adjective, or another adverb, such as quickly, delicately, beautifully, and valianty
Linking Verbs: Verbs linking states of being, linking subject to a word or subject. Examples are feel, look, seem, sounds, acted, was, and am
Helping verbs: Helping verbs help the main verb show tense or possibility. A helping verb AND a main verb create a verb phrase. Examples of a helping verb is is, were, was, may, do, been, being, be

Example Sentence: Identify the helping, linking, and adverb.
"Max was being awfully nice to the DJ."

The answer is:

Was is the Linking Verb,
being is the Helping Verb, and
awfully is the Adverb.

Conjunctions + Prepositional Phrases:
FANBOYS, space, and time.

Conjunctions: Words used to connect clauses or sentences or to coordinate words in the same clause. The FANBOYS acronym represents if, and, nor, but, or, yet, and so.
Preposition: A word that usually precedes a noun or pronoun to express a relation to another word or element through space or time. An example of a preposition is after, on, for, beside, with, and in.

Structure

Phrases, fragments, and clauses

These deal with the Structure of sentences, and contain parts of speech.

Use these helpful links for extra clarification:
Noun Phrase
Prepositional Phrase
Appositive Phrase

Noun Phrase: a noun phrase that includes a noun and the modifiers which distinguish it. Example: Larry the dog was the fastest dog in the park.
Prepositional Phrase: a phrase that begins with a preposition and usually ends with a noun. Example: At home, From Dallas, With me, Under there.
Appositive Phrase: A noun or noun phrase that renames a noun right beside it. Example: Lebron, a basketball player.
